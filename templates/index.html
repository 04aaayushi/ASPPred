One of the oldest and most elusive syndromes in medicine leading to deaths amongst hospitalized patients in the intensive care unit (ICU) is Sepsis, an ill-defined disease characterized by complex and time-dependent pathophysiological processes. Advances in the understanding of the pathophysiology of sepsis, which is characterized as a host reaction to infection involving not only the activation of pro- and anti-inﬂammatory responses but also modiﬁcations in non-immunological pathways (cardiovascular, autonomic, neurological, hormonal, metabolic and clotting), have led experts to revise the deﬁnitions.
